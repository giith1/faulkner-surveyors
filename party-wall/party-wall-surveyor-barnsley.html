What Is a Party Wall Notice

A Party Wall Notice is a legal heads-up to your neighbour that you’re about to start building something that might affect the wall you share or the ground near it. This isn’t just a courtesy call; it’s a requirement under the Party Wall Act 1996.

That wall you thought was “just part of the house” is technically shared property. So before you start drilling, digging, or demolishing, you’ll need to notify the other party involved. Otherwise, you could be staring down a legal dispute faster than you can say “load-bearing.”

The Party Wall Act 1996 Explained Simply

The Party Wall Act 1996 exists to keep things civil when neighbours share walls, fences, or foundations. It lays out clear steps for notifying neighbours, resolving disagreements, and keeping both properties protected during construction.

If your planned work will affect a shared wall or the ground near a neighbouring structure, the Act kicks in. And trust us it’s better to know before than after your neighbour calls their solicitor.

When Do You Need to Serve a Party Wall Notice?

According to the Act, you must serve a notice if you’re planning any of the following:

  • Excavating within 3 meters of a neighbour’s building

  • Digging within 6 meters if it’s deeper than their foundation

  • Building a wall on the boundary line

  • Cutting into or modifying a shared party wall

  • Demolishing or rebuilding a party wall

  • Inserting beams, supports, or steel into a party wall

Still unsure? If it sounds like your project falls into any of the above, don’t guess—get advice. Faulkners Surveyors is here to guide you through it all.

What Happens After You Serve the Notice?

Once the notice is served, your neighbour has 14 days to respond. Here’s where it gets interesting:

  • If they consent – Great! You’re good to go.

  • If they dissent – Time to appoint a Party Wall Surveyor.

If both parties agree, one surveyor can represent you both. If not, each side appoints their own. Either way, a Party Wall Award is drawn up setting out the rights and duties of everyone involved. It may cover access permissions, scaffolding requirements, work schedules, and who’s paying for what.
